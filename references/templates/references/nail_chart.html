Nail Chart

Nails are essential fasteners in woodworking, available in many sizes, types, and materials. Choosing the right nail helps ensure strength, appearance, and durability for your project.

Common Nail Types in Woodworking

Type Description Common Uses
Common Nail Thick shank, wide head, diamond point Framing, general construction, heavy-duty work
Box Nail Thinner shank than common nail Light framing, trim, exterior sheathing
Sinker Thinner than common, textured head, vinyl-coated Framing, easier to drive, less splitting
Finish Nail Small, rounded head, easily countersunk Trim, molding, finish carpentry
Casing Nail Larger finish nail, tapered head Exterior trim, door frames, casings
Brad Very thin, small head Light woodworking, small trim, delicate work
Duplex Nail Double head for easy removal Temporary construction, formwork
Roofing Nail Short, wide flat head Roofing felt, shingles, sheet metal
Ring-Shank/Spiral Nail Ridges or spiral on shank for holding power Subfloor, decking, siding, plywood
Tip: Use ring-shank or spiral nails for maximum holding in softwoods or plywood[4].

Brad Nails: Sizes, Applications & Best Practices

Brad nails are fine-gauge fasteners (typically 18 or 21 gauge) with a small head, designed for delicate woodworking tasks where a nearly invisible hole is desired.
[1] [2] [5]

Gauge Length Range Shank Diameter (in) Head Type Best For
21 ga (Micro Brads) 5/8" – 1-3/16" (15–30mm) ~0.033" (0.84mm) Very slight head Beadings, fine mouldings, delicate trim
18 ga (Standard Brads) 5/8" – 2" ~0.048" Small head Small/medium trim, paneling, casing, baseboards, crown molding, cabinet work, crafts
Applications:
  • Attaching lightweight trim and moldings without splitting the wood [2]
  • Panel installation, crafts, and fine woodworking where minimal surface filling is desired [5]
  • Cabinet assembly, jigs, fixtures, and stabilizing workpieces during glue-up [4]
Best Used For: Cases, small boxes, picture frames, furniture trim, and anywhere a clean, nearly invisible fastener is needed.
Tips:
  • Brad nails are less likely to split thin or delicate stock than finish nails or screws [6].
  • For best results, choose a brad length that penetrates the substrate by at least 1" [4].
  • Use a brad nailer for quick, accurate driving and to avoid bending small nails by hand [2].

Practical Tips for Nails in Woodworking
  • Use the shortest nail that will do the job—avoid splitting and excessive protrusion.
  • Blunt the tip of a nail with your hammer to reduce splitting in thin wood[4].
  • For structural framing, 8d and 16d nails (sinkers or common) cover most needs[6].
  • For outdoor or high-moisture projects, use galvanized or stainless steel nails for corrosion resistance[1].
  • Ring-shank and spiral nails provide extra holding power in softwoods and plywood[4].
