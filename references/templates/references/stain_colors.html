Wood Stain Colors Reference

Stains enhance the beauty and character of wood by adding color and highlighting grain. This guide covers common stain colors, types, application tips, and how wood species affects the final appearance.

Stain Types

  • Oil-Based Stains: Penetrate deeply, offer rich color, and are easy to blend. Require mineral spirits for cleanup.
  • Water-Based Stains: Dry quickly, have low odor, and clean up with water. Raise wood grain more than oil-based.
  • Gel Stains: Thick consistency, less likely to blotch, ideal for vertical surfaces or difficult woods.
  • Dye Stains: Fine pigments dissolve completely, providing vibrant, transparent color. Can be water- or alcohol-based.
  • Pre-Stain Conditioners: Not a stain, but used before staining to reduce blotching on softwoods and blotch-prone hardwoods.

How Wood Species Affects Stain Color

  • Softwoods (Pine, Fir, Spruce): Tend to absorb stain unevenly and may blotch. Use a pre-stain conditioner for best results.
  • Oak: Absorbs stain well and shows strong grain contrast. White oak is lighter and more golden; red oak is pinkish.
  • Maple, Birch, Cherry: Dense, tight grain absorbs stain unevenly and can blotch. Use gel stains or conditioners.
  • Walnut: Naturally dark; stains may deepen or mute the natural color.
  • Mahogany: Takes stain evenly, but often looks best with clear finishes to highlight natural color.
Tip: Always test your stain on a scrap of the same wood before finishing your project.

Stain Application Tips

  • Sand wood evenly to at least 180–220 grit before staining.
  • Remove all dust before applying stain.
  • Apply stain with a brush or clean cloth, following the grain.
  • Let the stain sit for 2–5 minutes, then wipe off excess with a clean cloth.
  • For deeper color, apply a second coat after the first dries.
  • Let stain dry thoroughly before applying a finish.
  • Use pre-stain conditioner on blotch-prone woods.
