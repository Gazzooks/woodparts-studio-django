Material Properties of Wood

Wood is a natural, anisotropic material whose properties vary by species, structure, and moisture content. Understanding these properties helps you select the right wood for each application.

Wood Structure & Composition

  • Cellular Structure: Wood consists mainly of cellulose, hemicellulose, and lignin, arranged in a cellular matrix for strength and flexibility. [1]
  • Types: Softwoods have simpler cell structures (mostly tracheids); hardwoods have more diverse cell types (vessels, fibers). [2]
  • Heartwood vs. Sapwood: Heartwood is dead, durable, and drier; sapwood is living and transports nutrients. [3]

Directional Properties (Anisotropy)

Wood properties vary by direction due to its structure:

  • Longitudinal: Along the grain (strongest, least shrinkage)
  • Radial: Perpendicular to growth rings (moderate shrinkage)
  • Tangential: Along growth rings (most shrinkage, weakest) [4]
Tip: Always consider grain direction in design and joinery for strength and stability.

Key Physical Properties

  • Density: Varies by species and moisture; denser woods are generally stronger and harder. [2]
  • Moisture Content: Wood absorbs/releases moisture from the air, affecting size (swelling/shrinkage), strength, and durability. [4]
  • Durability: Natural resistance to decay and insects depends on species and heartwood content; treatments can improve durability. [3]
  • Thermal Insulation: Wood is a good insulator compared to metals or concrete. [5]
  • Fire Properties: Wood chars predictably, providing some fire resistance; properties depend on density and moisture. [1]
  • Acoustics: Wood transmits sound differently based on density and structure, making it ideal for musical instruments. [3]

Key Mechanical Properties

Property Description Design Consideration Source
Elasticity Ability to return to original shape after bending Important for beams, joists, and springy parts [1]
Strength Resistance to breaking under load (bending, compression, tension, shear) Choose species with adequate strength for structural parts [6]
Hardness Resistance to surface wear and denting Matters for flooring, workbenches, tool handles [6]
Toughness Ability to absorb energy and resist shock Important for tool handles, sports equipment [4]
Workability Ease of cutting, shaping, gluing, and finishing Some woods are easier to machine and finish than others [3]
Creep Slow, permanent deformation under constant load Consider for shelving, long beams [5]
Note: Mechanical properties are affected by species, moisture content, grain direction, and presence of defects (knots, checks, etc.).

Special Properties

  • Hygroscopicity: Wood absorbs and releases moisture, causing dimensional changes (swelling/shrinking). [5]
  • Cleavability: Resistance to splitting; varies by species and grain. [4]
  • Stability: Some woods are more dimensionally stable than others under changing humidity. [2]
