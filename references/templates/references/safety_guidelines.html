Woodworking Safety Guidelines

Woodworking is rewarding and safe when you follow essential safety rules. These guidelines help protect you and others from injury, and ensure a productive, enjoyable workshop experience.

Personal Safety: Clothing, PPE, and Habits

  • Wear Safety Glasses at all times. Use a face shield for operations with flying debris or dust [PDF].
  • Hearing Protection: Use earplugs or earmuffs when using loud power tools (e.g., saws, planers) [PDF].
  • Respiratory Protection: Wear a dust mask or respirator, especially when sanding or working with fine dust or exotic woods [PDF].
  • Appropriate Clothing: Wear close-fitting clothes, remove jewelry, roll up sleeves, and tie back long hair to prevent entanglement [PDF].
  • Gloves: Do not wear gloves when operating machines with spinning blades—they can get caught. Use cut-resistant gloves only for handling sharp materials, not for machine work [PDF].
  • Footwear: Wear sturdy, closed-toe shoes (steel-toe preferred in professional shops) to protect against dropped tools and wood [PDF].
  • Stay Alert: Never use tools if tired, distracted, or under the influence of alcohol or drugs [PDF].

General Workshop Safety Rules

  1. Inspect the work area for hazards before starting. Keep the shop clean and free of clutter [PDF].
  2. Keep hands clean and dry for better grip and tool control [PDF].
  3. Always disconnect power before changing blades or bits, or servicing any tool [PDF].
  4. Use one heavy-duty extension cord for all portable tools to avoid trip hazards and ensure tools are unplugged when changing setups [PDF].
  5. Never use dull or damaged blades and bits. Sharp tools are safer and produce cleaner work [PDF].
  6. Check stock for nails, screws, or metal before cutting to avoid kickback and tool damage [PDF].
  7. Work against the cutter—feed wood in the correct direction for each tool [PDF].
  8. Never reach over or behind a running blade. Wait for blades to stop before clearing waste [PDF].
  9. Minimize distractions. Finish your cut or operation before turning your attention elsewhere [PDF].
  10. Be considerate of others; never endanger someone else with your actions [PDF].

Machine & Power Tool Safety

Tool/Machine Common Hazards Essential Safety Tips
Table Saw Kickback, blade contact Use riving knife and blade guard; stand to the side of the blade; use push sticks for small/narrow workpieces; never remove offcuts until blade stops [PDF].
Band Saw Blade contact Adjust blade guard close to work; keep fingers away from blade path; use push sticks for small parts [PDF].
Jointer Finger/hand injury, kickback Use push blocks; never joint stock shorter than recommended; keep hands away from cutterhead [PDF].
Router Flying debris, kickback Ensure bit is secure; use router shield; feed work against bit rotation; wear eye and hearing protection [PDF].
Sander Dust inhalation, contact injury Use dust collection and wear a mask; secure small parts; keep hands away from moving parts [PDF].
Tip: Only operate one machine at a time. Wait for all moving parts to stop before making adjustments [PDF].

Dust Control & Ventilation

  • Use dust collection systems and air filtration units to capture and remove wood dust at the source [PDF].
  • Wear a respirator or dust mask, especially when sanding or working with MDF, hardwoods, or exotic woods [PDF].
  • Regularly clean dust collectors and filters to prevent buildup and fire risk [PDF].
  • Keep the shop well-ventilated when using finishes, adhesives, or solvents [PDF].

Electrical Safety

  • Ensure all machines are properly grounded to reduce shock risk [PDF].
  • Regularly inspect cords and plugs for damage; replace as needed [PDF].
  • Do not overload circuits; use dedicated circuits for large equipment [PDF].
  • Avoid using extension cords for high-powered machines [PDF].

Emergency Preparedness

  • Keep a first aid kit and fire extinguisher accessible at all times [PDF].
  • Know emergency shutdown procedures for all machines [PDF].
  • Never leave a running machine unattended [PDF].
  • Install emergency stop buttons on large machines if possible [PDF].

Training & Attitude

  • Study and follow all safety instructions for tools and machines [PDF].
  • Attend safety training and refresher courses regularly [PDF].
  • Develop safe habits and always work carefully, even when no one is watching [PDF].
  • Encourage others to follow safety rules and set a good example [PDF].
