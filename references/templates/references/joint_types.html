Woodworking Joint Types

This reference guide shows common woodworking joints, their uses, and characteristics. Understanding different joints helps you choose the right technique for your project.

  • Basic Joints
  • Box & Dovetail Joints
  • Frame Joints
[Image]

Butt Joint

Strength: Low
Difficulty: Easy
Description:
The simplest joint where two pieces meet end to end or at a right angle. Usually reinforced with nails, screws, or dowels.
Common Uses:
Quick assembly, non-structural connections, rough framing
[Image]

Lap Joint

Strength: Medium
Difficulty: Easy
Description:
Overlapping two pieces by removing half the thickness from each. Stronger than a butt joint, but weaker than mortise and tenon.
Common Uses:
Frames, table legs, support braces, box construction
[Image]

Miter Joint

Strength: Low–Medium
Difficulty: Easy
Description:
Two pieces are cut at an angle (usually 45°) and joined to form a corner. Attractive, but not strong unless reinforced.
Common Uses:
Picture frames, decorative trim, boxes
[Image]

Dado Joint

Strength: Medium
Difficulty: Moderate
Description:
A groove cut across the grain of one piece to accept the end of another. Common in shelving and cabinetry.
Common Uses:
Bookcases, cabinets, drawer dividers
[Image]

Rabbet Joint

Strength: Medium
Difficulty: Moderate
Description:
A notch cut along the edge or end of a board to accept another piece. Used for box and cabinet backs, drawer bottoms.
Common Uses:
Cabinet and frame construction, drawer backs
[Image]

Biscuit Joint

Strength: Medium
Difficulty: Moderate
Description:
Uses a thin, oval-shaped "biscuit" inserted into slots cut in both pieces. Swells with glue to lock the joint.
Common Uses:
Panel glue-ups, casework, edge-joining boards
[Image]

Pocket Hole Joint

Strength: Medium
Difficulty: Easy
Description:
Angled holes are drilled to accept screws, joining pieces at right angles or edges. Fast, strong, and easy with a jig.
Common Uses:
Face frames, cabinets, quick assembly
[Image]

Box Joint (Finger Joint)

Strength: High
Difficulty: Moderate
Description:
Interlocking square "fingers" are cut into the ends of both pieces, then glued. Strong and decorative.
Common Uses:
Drawer boxes, decorative boxes, casework
[Image]

Dovetail Joint

Strength: Very High
Difficulty: Hard
Description:
Interlocking trapezoidal "tails and pins" resist pulling apart. Beautiful and extremely strong.
Common Uses:
Drawer construction, fine boxes, casework
[Image]

Half-Blind Dovetail

Strength: Very High
Difficulty: Hard
Description:
Like a dovetail, but the joint is only visible from one side. Used for drawer fronts where the joint should not show.
Common Uses:
Drawer fronts, fine cabinetry
[Image]

Mortise and Tenon Joint

Strength: Very High
Difficulty: Hard
Description:
A tenon (tongue) on one piece fits into a mortise (hole) in the other. Classic, strong, and versatile.
Common Uses:
Chairs, tables, doors, frames, furniture
[Image]

Bridle Joint

Strength: High
Difficulty: Moderate
Description:
Similar to mortise and tenon, but open on three sides. Easier to cut, strong for frames and legs.
Common Uses:
Frames, legs, rails, load-bearing joints
[Image]

Dowel Joint

Strength: Medium–High
Difficulty: Moderate
Description:
Round wooden dowels are inserted into matching holes in both pieces. Good strength and alignment.
Common Uses:
Frames, panels, furniture, chairs
[Image]

Corner Bridle Joint

Strength: High
Difficulty: Moderate
Description:
A variation of the bridle joint, used at corners for strong, load-bearing frames.
Common Uses:
Table legs, bench legs, frames
