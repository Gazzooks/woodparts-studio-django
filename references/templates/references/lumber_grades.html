Lumber Grades Reference

Understanding lumber grades helps you select the right wood for your project, whether for structural framing, fine woodworking, or cabinetry. Lumber is graded based on appearance, strength, and intended use. Below are the most common grading systems for softwoods and hardwoods.

Softwood Lumber Grades (Construction Lumber)

Grade Description Common Uses
SELECT STRUCTURAL Highest quality, fewest defects, highest strength Beams, posts, load-bearing framing
NO. 1 (Construction) High quality, few knots, strong Framing, joists, rafters
NO. 2 (Standard) More knots, some defects allowed, still strong General framing, studs, plates
NO. 3 (Utility) More defects, larger knots, not for structural use Crates, bracing, non-load-bearing
STUD Specifically graded for vertical use in walls Wall studs
CONSTRUCTION HEART Primarily heartwood, strong and stable Framing, load-bearing
CONSTRUCTION COMMON Mix of heartwood and sapwood, more defects General construction
Note: Softwood grades are stamped on each piece and may include additional marks for kiln drying (KD), pressure treatment, or species.

Hardwood Lumber Grades (NHLA Rules)

Grade Description Yield (%) Common Uses
FAS (Firsts and Seconds) Best quality, long clear faces, minimal defects 83⅓%+ Fine furniture, cabinetry, millwork
FAS 1-Face (F1F) One face FAS, reverse face #1 Common 83⅓%+ Cabinet doors, panels
Select One face nearly FAS, reverse face #1 Common 83⅓%+ Furniture, visible parts
#1 Common Good quality, shorter clear faces, more defects 66⅔%+ Furniture frames, panels, flooring
#2 Common More defects, smaller clear areas 50%+ Cabinet frames, utility furniture
#3A & #3B Common Lowest grade, significant defects 33⅓%+ Boxes, crates, utility use
Note: Hardwood grades are based on the percentage of clear, defect-free wood in a board. Thickness, width, and length requirements apply to each grade.
Other Grading Terms & Stamps
  • S4S: Surfaced Four Sides (planed smooth on all sides)
  • S2S: Surfaced Two Sides (planed smooth on two faces, rough edges)
  • Rough: Not planed or surfaced, as sawn from the mill
  • KD: Kiln Dried (moisture reduced for stability)
  • PT: Pressure Treated (for outdoor use)
  • MC: Moisture Content (often marked as a percentage)
Tip: Always check the lumber stamp for grade, species, moisture content, and certification (e.g., FSC, SFI).
