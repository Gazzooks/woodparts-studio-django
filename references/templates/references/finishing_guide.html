Application Techniques

Brushing: The most common method using a high-quality brush.
Best for: Polyurethane, varnish, shellac, water-based finishes
  • Choose a high-quality natural or synthetic brush appropriate for your finish
  • Load the brush properly - about 1/3 of the bristle length
  • Apply with the grain using long, even strokes
  • Maintain a wet edge to prevent lap marks
  • Allow proper drying time between coats
  • Sand lightly between coats with fine sandpaper (320-400 grit)
Wiping: Applying finish with a cloth for a thin, even coat.
Best for: Oils, Danish oil, oil/varnish blends, wipe-on poly
  • Use a lint-free cloth (old t-shirts work well)
  • Apply finish liberally to the surface
  • Wipe off excess after a short period (follow product instructions)
  • Allow to dry completely between coats
  • Multiple thin coats are better than fewer thick coats
  • Sand very lightly between coats if needed
Spraying: Using spray equipment for a smooth, even finish without brush marks.
Best for: Lacquer, water-based finishes, conversion varnish
  • Ensure proper ventilation and wear appropriate protective equipment
  • Thin finish according to manufacturer's directions if needed
  • Keep the gun perpendicular to the surface at a consistent distance
  • Use overlapping passes for even coverage
  • Apply multiple light coats rather than one heavy coat
  • Allow proper flash time between coats
Padding: A French polishing technique using a pad to apply thin coats.
Best for: Shellac (traditional French polish)
  • Create a pad with a lint-free cloth wrapped around wool or cotton
  • Charge the pad with shellac and a small amount of oil
  • Apply in circular or figure-eight motions
  • Use light pressure and keep the pad moving
  • Build up many thin coats
  • Allow proper drying between sessions
Dipping: Immersing the entire piece or parts in finish.
Best for: Small items, production work
  • Prepare a container large enough for the items
  • Fill with appropriate finish
  • Submerge items completely
  • Remove and allow excess to drain
  • Hang to dry in a dust-free environment

Wood Preparation Steps

  1. Complete All Construction First: Finish all woodworking operations including joinery, shaping, and carving before starting the finishing process.
  2. Sanding Progression: Start with coarser grits and work up to finer grits. A typical progression might be: 80 → 120 → 150 → 180 → 220 grit. For some finishes, you may need to go finer to 320 or 400 grit.
    Always sand with the grain. Cross-grain scratches will show in the final finish.
  3. Raise the Grain: For water-based finishes, raise the grain first by lightly wetting the surface with water, letting it dry, then lightly sanding with the final grit.
    This prevents the grain from raising when applying water-based finish.
  4. Surface Cleaning: Remove all dust using a vacuum, compressed air, and/or tack cloth. Mineral spirits can be used to check for missed spots and to reveal glue residue.
  5. Wood Conditioning: For blotch-prone woods (pine, cherry, maple, birch), apply a pre-stain wood conditioner or washcoat of thinned shellac before staining.
  6. Filling the Grain: For open-grained woods (oak, ash, mahogany), consider using wood filler or grain filler for a smooth finish.
    Apply filler after staining but before applying finish if you want the pores to show the stain color.
  7. Final Wipe Down: Just before finishing, wipe the surface with a clean, lint-free cloth to remove any remaining dust.

Troubleshooting Common Finishing Problems

Problem Possible Causes Solutions
Bubbles in Finish Shaking the finish, applying too quickly, contaminated surface, excessive brush agitation Stir don't shake, apply more slowly, clean surface thoroughly, use proper brush technique, thin the finish
Orange Peel (Rough Texture) Spraying too far away, finish too thick, improper atomization Adjust spray distance, thin the finish, check spray equipment settings
Fish Eye (Small Circular Depressions) Silicone or oil contamination on the wood surface Clean thoroughly with solvent, use fish-eye eliminator additive, apply shellac sealer coat
Blushing (Milky Appearance) Moisture trapped in the finish, high humidity during application Apply in lower humidity, add retarder, sand and recoat, apply thinner coats
Runs and Sags Applying too much finish, uneven application, vertical surfaces Apply thinner coats, maintain even application, watch for runs while finish is wet
Brush Marks Poor quality brush, finish drying too quickly, finish too thick Use better brush, add retarder, thin the finish, sand between coats
Dust Nibs Dust in the air settling on wet finish Clean workspace, use tack cloth, sand between coats with fine paper (400+ grit)
Blotchy Stain Uneven wood porosity, inadequate surface preparation Use pre-stain conditioner, apply washcoat of shellac, use gel stain
Peeling or Poor Adhesion Contaminated surface, incompatible finishes, moisture issues Clean thoroughly, sand properly, ensure finish compatibility, check moisture content
Finish Won't Dry Temperature too low, incompatible products, oil or silicone contamination Increase temperature, verify product compatibility, clean surface thoroughly
Streaky Finish Uneven application, poor surface preparation, incompatible products Apply evenly, prepare surface properly, ensure product compatibility
Cracking or Crazing Applied too thick, temperature changes, incompatible layers Apply thinner coats, maintain stable temperature, ensure compatibility between coats
